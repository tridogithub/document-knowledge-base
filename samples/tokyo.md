## What are the absolute must-see attractions in Tokyo?

Tokyo's non-negotiables come down to three experiences: Shibuya Crossing at night, Senso-ji Temple in Asakusa before 8am (before tour groups arrive), and at least one neighborhood deep-dive — Shimokitazawa, Yanaka, or Koenji — where Tokyo actually feels like a city people live in, not a theme park. Everything else is optional depending on your interests.

### Shibuya & Harajuku — Tokyo's Fashion and Energy Hub

**Shibuya Crossing** (35-2 Udagawacho, Shibuya) is best experienced from above: grab a window seat at the Starbucks overlooking the crossing (free, just buy a coffee), or pay ¥2,700 (~$18) for the Shibuya Sky rooftop observation deck. Ground level is chaotic and great for 5 minutes; the views are where the magic actually is.

**Harajuku's Takeshita Street**: Instagram-worthy but genuinely touristy on weekends. Go before 11am or on a Tuesday. The crepe shops are legitimately good ($4–6). For something more interesting, exit the other side of Harajuku Station toward Omotesando — Tokyo's answer to the Champs-Élysées, with better architecture.

**Meiji Shrine** (1-1 Yoyogikamizonocho, Shibuya): Free entry. The forested walk-in alone — 10 minutes through ancient trees in the middle of a city of 13 million — is worth 45 minutes of your trip. Skip the ¥1,000 fortune paper though (it's for tourists).

*Insider tip:* Yoyogi Park is directly adjacent. Sunday afternoons bring out rockabilly dancers, cosplay groups, and amateur bands near the south entrance. Completely free, completely surreal.

### Asakusa — Old Tokyo Without the Kitsch

**Senso-ji Temple** (2-3-1 Asakusa, Taito): Tokyo's oldest temple, built in 645 AD. Free entry. The key is timing: arrive before 8am and you'll have the Nakamise shopping street and main hall to yourself. After 10am, it's bus tours. The 5-story Asakusa pagoda is best photographed from Sumida Park across the street.

The streets around Senso-ji — particularly Nakamise Dori and the covered shopping arcades heading east — still feel like 1960s Tokyo. Ningyo-yaki (small cakes filled with red bean paste, ¥100–150 each) are made fresh while you watch.

**Sumida River cruise**: A 35-minute low bridge cruise from Asakusa to Hamarikyu costs ¥1,320 (~$9). The views are industrial and interesting, not scenic-beautiful. Better value than most "river cruise" experiences in other cities, and the Hamarikyu gardens at the end (¥300 entry) are genuinely beautiful.

### Akihabara — Electronics and Anime Without Getting Ripped Off

Skip the big storefronts on the main drag (prices are tourist-marked) and go straight to the upper floors of Yodobashi Camera or the backstreet multi-story buildings. The real Akihabara is upstairs: floors 3–7 of random buildings have vintage game consoles, obscure manga, and the capsule toy machines (gashapon) at actual prices.

**Nakano Broadway** (5-52-15 Nakano): 20 minutes by train from Shinjuku, and significantly less crowded than Akihabara. Better for rare finds. The Mandarake chain's Nakano flagship has 4 floors of second-hand anime merchandise, vintage manga, and figures — often at 30–50% below new price.

## What neighborhoods should I prioritize in Tokyo?

The neighborhood you stay in shapes your entire experience. Shinjuku gives you access to everything but feels overwhelming. Asakusa feels authentically old Tokyo and has cheaper hotels. Shimokitazawa is where locals actually go — but it takes longer to get anywhere from there.

| Neighborhood | Best For | Budget Signal | Skip If... |
| --- | --- | --- | --- |
| **Shinjuku** | First-timers, nightlife, transport hub | Mid–high (¥8,000–25,000/night) | You want quiet |
| **Asakusa** | Old Tokyo vibes, budget accommodation | Budget–mid (¥3,500–12,000/night) | You hate tourist crowds by day |
| **Shimokitazawa** | Vintage shopping, live music, local vibe | Budget (many hostels, ¥2,500–4,000) | You need quick access to major sites |
| **Nakameguro** | Cafés, boutiques, cherry blossom canal | Mid–high (¥10,000–20,000/night) | You want big attractions nearby |
| **Ueno/Akihabara** | Budget travelers, museums, anime | Budget (¥2,500–8,000/night) | You're not into museums or anime |
| **Ginza** | Luxury shopping, Michelin dining | Luxury (¥25,000–80,000+/night) | You're on a budget |

### Yanaka — Tokyo's Best Hidden Neighborhood

Yanaka (accessible from Nippori or Nezu stations) is what most of Tokyo looked like before the 1964 Olympics rebuilding. Streets are narrow, wooden buildings are still standing, cats sit in doorways. **Yanaka Ginza shopping street** has handmade goods, local snacks, and almost no other foreign tourists on weekday mornings. Budget 2–3 hours and combine with Nezu Shrine (free, beautiful azalea garden in April–May).

## What unique Tokyo experiences can't I find anywhere else?

Beyond the obvious, Tokyo has experiences that genuinely don't exist anywhere else: real-life Mario Kart racing through Shibuya streets, teamLab's walk-through digital art that reacts to your body, a sumo tournament (only 3 times/year in Tokyo), and a parasitology museum with a 9-meter tapeworm in a jar. Not all are for everyone — but these are the things people remember.

- **teamLab Planets TOKYO** (6-1-16 Toyosu, Koto): ¥3,800–4,800 (~$25–32) depending on the day. Barefoot walk through knee-deep water with digital koi, mirrored rooms, flower projections. Book 2–3 weeks ahead — it sells out. Better on weekdays. Rating: 4.66/5 from 9,000+ reviews on GetYourGuide.
- **Mario Kart Racing** (Shibuya): ~¥6,000–8,000/person (~$40–54). Dress as your favorite Nintendo character, drive through real Tokyo traffic including Shibuya Crossing. Genuinely illegal-feeling and totally legal. Book 2 weeks ahead, sells out constantly.
- **Sumo Tournament at Ryogoku Kokugikan** (1-3-28 Yokoami, Sumida): Tokyo hosts 3 tournaments/year (January, May, September). Front-row box seats: ¥38,000 (~$255). Rear balcony seats: ¥2,200 (~$15). Book through the Japan Sumo Association website as soon as dates drop — good seats are gone within hours.
- **Meguro Parasitological Museum** (4-1-1 Shimomeguro, Meguro): Free entry. One room, two floors, over 300 specimens. The 8.8-meter-long tapeworm extracted from a man's intestines in 1959 is exhibit #7. Strange, educational, unforgettable. Usually empty of tourists.
- **Gotokuji Temple — Lucky Cat Temple** (2-24-7 Gotokuji, Setagaya): Free entry. Hundreds of porcelain maneki-neko (beckoning cat) figures covering the grounds. Almost no tourists on weekdays. The walk through the quiet residential streets of Setagaya to get there is half the appeal.

## How much does it cost to visit Tokyo? (Real 2026 numbers)

Tokyo is cheaper than [London](https://skysonar.com/en-us/flights/from-london-lon "Find flights from London") or [New York](https://skysonar.com/en-us/flights/from-new-york-nyc "Find flights from New York") for most travelers in 2026, thanks to the weak yen. Budget travelers spending ¥11,000/day (~$74) can eat well, use transit freely, and see most major attractions. Mid-range travelers at $148/day get comfortable hotels, nice restaurants, and paid experiences. Here's the realistic breakdown:

| Category | Budget ($74/day) | Mid-Range ($148/day) | Luxury ($280+/day) |
| --- | --- | --- | --- |
| Accommodation | Hostel/capsule: $20–28 | Business hotel: $70–100 | Boutique/4-star: $150–300 |
| Food | Konbini + ramen: $15–20 | Sit-down + 1 nice dinner: $35–55 | Kaiseki/omakase: $80–150 |
| Transport | ¥600 day pass: ~$4 | IC card (as-you-go): $8–12 | IC card + occasional taxi: $15–25 |
| Activities | Mostly free shrines: $5–10 | 1–2 paid attractions: $20–35 | Premium experiences: $50–100 |

**2026 money-saving notes:**

- Exit tax has tripled to ¥3,000 (~$20) per person — included in your outbound flight ticket automatically
- Tax-free shopping: tourists avoid Japan's 10% consumption tax on purchases over ¥5,000 — but from 2026, you pay upfront and claim at the airport (change from previous immediate exemption)
- 7-Eleven ATMs: no foreign card fees. Withdraw larger amounts less frequently to minimize per-transaction costs
- Free observation decks: Tokyo Metropolitan Government Building (Shinjuku) — free. Tokyo Tower's main deck is ¥1,200 and the view is worse than Skytree

## What's the best 2-day itinerary for Tokyo first-timers?

Two days is enough to see the essential Tokyo without rushing. The key is grouping by geography — each of these day plans requires minimal transit. Don't try to mix Asakusa and Shibuya in the same morning; the city is enormous and transit between districts takes 30–45 minutes each way.

### Day 1: West Tokyo (Shinjuku → Harajuku → Shibuya)

- **8:30am** — Meiji Shrine (45 min, free). The forest walk alone is worth it. Go early to beat tour groups.
- **10:00am** — Harajuku/Takeshita Street (30 min) + Omotesando avenue (1 hour, great cafés)
- **12:30pm** — Lunch in Harajuku. Standing sushi at Uobei Shibuya Dogenzaka: ¥100–200/plate on a conveyor, no English menus, point and eat
- **2:00pm** — Shibuya Crossing from Shibuya Sky observation deck (¥2,700) or Starbucks window (free)
- **4:00pm** — Shinjuku: Kabukicho entertainment district, Golden Gai (150+ tiny bars), Omoide Yokocho (Memory Lane) for yakitori under the train tracks
- **7:00pm** — Dinner in Golden Gai. Budget ¥2,000–3,000 for drinks and bar snacks in a bar that fits 8 people maximum

### Day 2: East Tokyo (Asakusa → Akihabara → Ueno)

- **7:30am** — Senso-ji Temple (free). The only time to see it without crowds. Morning light on the pagoda is the best photo in Tokyo.
- **9:00am** — Breakfast in Asakusa: Nakamise shops open around 9, or find a traditional Japanese breakfast set at a nearby teishoku restaurant (~¥800–1,200)
- **10:30am** — Sumida River walk toward Skytree (30 min walk, free). Tokyo Skytree tickets: ¥2,100–3,100 depending on floor. Views are excellent. Skip on cloudy days.
- **1:00pm** — Akihabara: focus on upper floors of buildings, not storefronts. Lunch: any curry house on the main street (~¥900–1,200)
- **3:00pm** — Ueno Park (free) + Tokyo National Museum (¥1,000) if you like ancient Japanese art. Otherwise, Ueno Zoo (¥600) for families.
- **6:00pm** — Dinner: Yurakucho area under the train tracks. Dozens of small restaurants serving sashimi, grilled fish, and cold beer. Budget ¥2,000–3,500.

## When is the best time to visit Tokyo?

Cherry blossom season (late March–early April) is genuinely spectacular and genuinely overcrowded — hotel prices spike 50–100%. If you want to see sakura without the chaos, choose a weekday and go to a less-famous spot like Shinjuku Gyoen (¥500 entry, limits crowds) rather than Ueno Park. June (rainy season) has lower prices and fewer tourists; it's actually a fine time to visit if you pack accordingly.

| Period | Pros | Cons | Budget Impact |
| --- | --- | --- | --- |
| **Jan–Feb** | Cheapest hotels, fewest tourists, sumo tournament (Jan) | Cold (0–10°C), New Year week expensive | \-30% vs. peak |
| **Late Mar–Apr** | Cherry blossoms, warm weather, festivals | Crowds, expensive hotels, sold-out attractions | +50–100% peak |
| **May** | Great weather, sumo tournament | Golden Week (late April–early May) is extremely busy | +20% Golden Week |
| **Jun** | Low prices, fewer tourists, hydrangeas | Rainy season (frequent showers) | \-20% vs. peak |
| **Jul–Aug** | Festivals, fireworks, full summer energy | Very hot and humid (30–36°C), crowded | Standard pricing |
| **Oct–Nov** | Perfect weather, fall foliage, sumo tournament (Sep) | Popular — book accommodation early | +15–25% vs. summer |

## What are the best day trips from Tokyo?

Tokyo's rail network makes day trips genuinely easy. Kamakura (50 minutes by train, ¥920 each way) has the Great Buddha, 65 temples, and actual ocean views — it's the day trip with the best variety and most manageable crowds. Nikko (2 hours, covered by JR Pass) has over-the-top ornate shrines and mountains. Hakone (85 minutes, Romancecar train: ¥2,470) gives you Mt. Fuji views and onsen — skip it if Mt. Fuji is obscured by clouds (check [Japan National Tourism Organization's forecast](https://www.jnto.go.jp/) the night before).

- **Kamakura**: Great Buddha (¥300), 2-hour hiking trail between temples, fresh sashimi at Yuigahama beach restaurants (~¥1,500–2,500). Best on weekdays. Total day budget: $40–60.
- **Nikko**: Tosho-gu Shrine complex (¥1,300), waterfalls, mountain cedar forest. Take the 7am shinkansen for 3+ hours before tourist buses arrive.
- **Hakone**: Mt. Fuji views (weather-dependent), rope-way over volcanic crater, onsen hotels. Hakone Free Pass (¥5,000–6,500) covers most transport.

## What's new in Tokyo for 2026?

Three genuinely significant openings in 2026 are worth knowing before you plan:

- **Edo-Tokyo Museum reopens** (2-3 Yokoami, Sumida): Closed since 2022 for renovation, this 7-story museum documenting Tokyo's history from the Edo period through World War II reopens in 2026. Previously one of the best museums in the city; the renovation adds a permanent 2020 Olympics section.
- **PokéPark in Odaiba**: Full-scale Pokémon theme park opening in 2026. Expected ¥3,500–5,000 ticket prices. Advance booking will be mandatory — check the official Pokémon website when announced.
- **Luxury hotel openings in Akasaka**: Several international brands opening in the Akasaka/Roppongi area, which means competition is driving prices down at existing properties in Shinjuku and Shibuya.

## How do I get from the airport to central Tokyo?

Most international flights land at Narita (NRT, 60km from central Tokyo) or Haneda (HND, 15km from central Tokyo). Haneda is significantly more convenient. If your routing gives you a choice, choose Haneda.

- **Narita to Shinjuku**: Narita Express (N'EX): ¥3,070 (~$21), 80 minutes. Limousine bus: ¥3,200, 90–120 minutes (traffic-dependent). Avoid taxis — they cost ¥20,000–30,000+.
- **Haneda to Shinjuku**: Airport Limousine Bus: ¥1,400, 45–60 minutes. Tokyo Monorail + Yamanote Line: ¥700, 40 minutes. Much easier than Narita.
- **IC Card (Suica or Pasmo)**: Get one at any airport station on arrival (¥1,000 initial charge includes ¥500 refundable deposit). Use it on every train, bus, and at most convenience stores. Eliminates ticket-buying for the entire trip.

## Frequently Asked Questions — Things to Do in Tokyo

### How many days do you need in Tokyo?

3–4 days covers the major highlights: Shibuya, Shinjuku, Asakusa, Akihabara, and 1–2 neighborhood deep-dives. 5–7 days allows day trips to Kamakura or Nikko and slower exploration. First-time visitors consistently underestimate how large Tokyo is — a "quick visit" to Harajuku and Asakusa in the same morning means 45 minutes on the train each way.

### Is Tokyo safe for solo travelers?

Tokyo is routinely ranked among the safest major cities in the world. Violent crime against tourists is exceptionally rare. The main practical risks are: getting lost in the metro (get Google Maps offline before landing), overspending at tourist-targeted shops in Asakusa and Akihabara, and underestimating summer heat in July–August. Solo travelers, including solo women, report feeling safer here than in most Western capitals.

### What should I know about Tokyo etiquette before visiting?

Key rules: no eating while walking (except at festivals and food stalls where vendors are present), don't tip (it's considered rude), stand on the left side of escalators in Tokyo (right side in Osaka), don't speak loudly on trains, and remove shoes when entering traditional restaurants, ryokans, and some guesthouses. Carrying a handkerchief is useful — many public restrooms lack hand dryers.

### What's overrated in Tokyo?

Tokyo Tower (Skytree has better views and costs similar), teamLab Planets on weekends (1–2 hour queues), Tsukiji outer market before 9am (the famous tuna auctions moved to Toyosu in 2018), and any "ninja restaurant" or samurai experience marketed primarily in English. The actual samurai museums (Samurai Museum in Shinjuku, ¥1,900) are genuinely good though.

### Do I need to speak Japanese to visit Tokyo?

No — Tokyo has significantly improved English signage since the 2020 Olympics, and major train stations, airports, and tourist areas have English on nearly all signs. Translation apps (Google Translate camera mode) handle menus. Where you'll struggle: rural areas of Tokyo, small local restaurants without picture menus, and calling ahead to reserve at traditional restaurants. Most issues can be solved by pointing, showing your phone, or using a booking app.

### Can I get around Tokyo without a JR Pass?

Yes, if you're staying in Tokyo. The JR Pass is primarily valuable for inter-city travel (Tokyo–Kyoto–Osaka). For Tokyo-only travel, a Suica or Pasmo IC card at ¥170–310 per ride covers the metro, JR lines, and buses. A 24-hour subway pass (¥600) covers unlimited Tokyo Metro rides. The JR Pass breaks even only if you're doing at least one long-distance shinkansen journey — calculate your specific routes on [Hyperdia](https://www.hyperdia.com/) before buying.

### What's the cheapest time to fly to Tokyo?

January and February (post-New Year) are cheapest for flights — 20–30% below peak season. June is also good but check airline schedules as rainy season affects some operations. Avoid: late March through Golden Week (April 29–May 5), Obon (mid-August), and the New Year period (December 28–January 4). These windows see flight prices double and accommodation sell out months in advance.

### Is the food in Tokyo really that good?

Yes — and affordable. Tokyo has more Michelin stars than any other city in the world (230+), but you don't need them. A bowl of ramen at a standing counter costs ¥800–1,200 (~$5–8) and will be better than 90% of ramen you've had elsewhere. Convenience store onigiri (¥150) is genuinely good. The floor of any Isetan or Takashimaya basement food hall has more variety than most cities' entire culinary scenes. Budget travelers eat exceptionally well here.
